// Strength Reference

Strength Block — Cues & Progression

Lower body strength runs on Tuesdays after the Olympic work. Upper body strength runs on Thursdays. Both days open with Box Jumps as a CNS primer. Volume builds weeks 1–6, then drops sharply in weeks 7–8 to protect legs for race day.

Box Jumps — Athleticism Primer
Done at the start of every strength block — both Tuesday and Thursday. CNS primer before heavy work.
  • Why first: Box jumps activate fast-twitch muscle fibers and prime the nervous system. Heavy squats and rows done after jumps produce more force than without this primer. Never do them after fatiguing work.
  • Setup: Stand close to the box. Hinge slightly at the hips, swing arms, then drive up explosively through your whole foot. Land softly with knees bent — absorb the landing through the whole leg, not just the knee joint.
  • Full hip extension: At the top of the jump, fully extend your hips — stand tall on the box. Cutting the jump short by landing in a crouch is a missed rep.
  • Step down, don't jump down: Step off the box one foot at a time. Jumping down repeatedly accumulates eccentric stress that will affect your running.
  • Progression: Wks 1–3: 4×4 at knee height. Wks 4–5: 4×5, increase box height if comfortable. Wk 6: 5×5 max height. Wks 7–8: drop to 3×3–4, prioritize sharpness over volume.
  • Rest between sets: 90 seconds minimum. These are power sets — full recovery between each. If rep 4 looks slower than rep 1, rest longer.
Front Squat — Lower Body Anchor
Tuesday. Already trained in the Olympic block — the strength version uses heavier load and strict tempo.
  • Why front squat (not back squat): The front squat rack position is identical to the clean catch. Building front squat strength directly increases how much you can clean. It also demands more quad dominance and upright torso — better for runners.
  • Tempo: 3 seconds down, 1 second pause at the bottom, drive up. The pause eliminates the stretch reflex and forces true strength from the bottom position.
  • Elbows up: If your elbows drop during the descent, the bar rolls forward and the lift fails. Practice keeping elbows parallel to the floor even as the load increases.
  • Depth standard: Hip crease below the top of the knee on every rep. If depth suffers when weight increases, the weight is too heavy — strip plates and build back.
  • Progression: Wks 1–3: 4×5 building load. Wks 4–6: 5×5, work up to a heavy set (peak in wk 6). Wks 7–8: 3×3 then 1–2×3 maintenance only.
Bulgarian Split Squat — Single-Leg Strength
Tuesday. The most direct strength transfer to running — every running stride is a single-leg loading event.
  • Setup: Rear foot rests on a bench (laces down or toes down — toes down is harder on the hip flexor, laces down is easier). Front foot far enough forward that your shin stays roughly vertical at the bottom.
  • Depth: Rear knee just above the floor. If it touches the floor, that is the full range — own it. Do not cut the depth to add weight.
  • Drive: Push through the front heel to stand. Imagine trying to push the floor away from you. The front glute should be doing most of the work — if it is all quad, your foot is too close to the bench.
  • Asymmetry alert: Your weaker leg is your limiter. Do not compensate by leaning toward the strong side. If one side is significantly weaker, start every set with the weak leg.
  • Progression: Wk 1: bodyweight. Wk 2: light DBs (10–20 lbs). Wks 3–6: build load systematically. Wks 7–8: reduce sets, maintain load.
  • Mobility note: Tight hip flexors (from running) make the rear hip uncomfortable. The couch stretch on recovery days directly addresses this.
Romanian Deadlift — Posterior Chain
Tuesday. Builds the hamstrings and glutes that power your running stride and protect your knees.
  • Hinge, do not squat: The RDL is a pure hip hinge. Push your hips back until you feel a strong hamstring pull — the knees have a slight bend but do not drive forward. This is not a squat.
  • Bar path: The bar (or DBs) drags along your legs the entire way down and back up. If the bar is swinging away from your body, the load is in the wrong muscle group.
  • Tempo: 3-second descent minimum. The eccentric (lowering) phase is where the hamstring strength is built. Do not drop the weight — control it all the way down.
  • Range of motion: Lower until you feel a strong hamstring pull — for most people this is mid-shin. Do not force the bar to the floor if your back rounds. Rounded lower back in an RDL can irritate the lumbar discs.
  • Connection to running: Strong hamstrings from RDLs reduce the risk of hamstring strain — the most common running injury. They also increase hip extension power, which directly increases your top speed.
  • Progression: Wks 1–2: 3×10 establishing form. Wks 3–6: 4×8 building load. Wks 7–8: reduce to 2×6 maintenance.
Pendlay Row — Explosive Back Strength
Thursday. The Pendlay row is not a bent-over row. Every rep starts from a dead stop on the floor — this is what makes it powerful.
  • Setup: Barbell on the floor. Hinge to horizontal — your torso is parallel to the floor, not 45°. This is the hardest part: most people cheat by raising their torso to make the pull easier.
  • The pull: Explosively row the bar to your lower chest or upper abdomen. The bar should travel in a straight vertical line. Think "pull the bar through the floor" rather than "lift it up."
  • Dead stop: Set the bar completely down between every rep — it should make contact with the floor and go still before the next pull. This eliminates momentum and maximises the power development stimulus.
  • Why it matters: The explosive hip-back connection in a Pendlay row directly mirrors the second pull of the clean. Building this row makes your cleans stronger.
  • Progression: Wks 1–2: 4×6 establishing baseline. Wks 3–4: 4×6 adding load. Wks 5–6: 5×5 building to a heavy set. Wks 7–8: taper to 3×3 then 1–2×3.
Pull-Ups & Single-Arm DB Press
Thursday. Pull-ups build the lats that stabilize the clean catch. The single-arm press builds the overhead strength the snatch demands.
  • Pull-Up standards: Dead hang start — shoulder blades depressed, arms fully extended. Pull until chin clears the bar. Lower with control (2–3 seconds). No kipping.
  • Pull-Up scaling: Band assistance if you cannot complete 5 clean reps. Add weight (vest or DB between feet) if you can do 12+ reps easily. Progress by adding reps or weight week over week.
  • Pull-Up and the clean: The lats are the muscles that keep the bar close to your body during the pull. Weak lats = bar looping away from the body = failed clean. Pull-up strength fixes this directly.
  • Single-Arm DB Press — why single-arm: The unilateral load forces your core to resist rotation on every rep. This is more specific to the snatch overhead lockout than a bilateral press.
  • Single-Arm Press technique: Seated or standing. Press directly overhead — no forward tilt, no lateral trunk lean. If you are leaning to the pressing side, the weight is too heavy. Control the lowering phase (2 seconds down).
  • Progression: Wks 1–3: 3×10/side at moderate load. Wks 4–6: 4×8/side building to a challenging weight. Wks 7–8: reduce to 2–3 sets, stop well short of failure.
Lateral Raises & Ring Push-Ups
Thursday. Lateral raises build the medial deltoid. Ring push-ups build chest, triceps, and rotator cuff simultaneously.
  • Lateral Raise technique: Slight bend in the elbow throughout. Lead with the elbows — imagine pouring a jug of water at arm's length. Raise to shoulder height only — going higher recruits traps, not delts.
  • Lateral Raise tempo: Raise in 1 second, lower in 3 seconds. The slow lowering (eccentric) is where the medial deltoid hypertrophy stimulus lives. Do not just drop the weight.
  • Lateral Raise and overhead work: A strong medial deltoid reduces shoulder impingement risk under the pressing demands of the snatch. Think of these as shoulder injury prevention as much as aesthetics.
  • Ring Push-Up setup: Rings at 12–18 inches off the floor for most people. Body in a straight plank from heels to head — no sagging hips, no raised hips. Lower with control.
  • Ring Push-Up top position: At the top, actively rotate the rings outward (turn your thumbs away from each other). This external rotation at the top fires the rotator cuff and builds shoulder stability specific to the snatch overhead position.
  • Ring Push-Up progression: Raise the rings to make easier, lower them to make harder. As the program advances, lower the rings by 1–2 inches per week. In peak week the rings should be as low as you can manage while keeping a rigid plank.
Strength Periodization — 8-Week Overview
How the strength volume and intensity changes across the 8 weeks.
  • Weeks 1–3 (Foundation): Learn the movements. Conservative loads, focus on technique and tempo. Box jumps 4×4. Lower: 4×5 FS, 3×8 BSS/RDL. Upper: 4×6 row, 3×reps pull-ups, 3×10 press, 3×12 laterals, 3×near-failure rings.
  • Weeks 4–5 (Build): Add a set and increase loads across all movements. Box jumps 4×5. Lower: 5×5 FS, 4×8 BSS/RDL. Upper: 5×5 row, 4×reps pull-ups, 4×8 press, 4×12–15 laterals, 4×near-failure rings.
  • Week 6 (Peak): Highest volume and intensity of the program. Box jumps 5×5. All strength movements reach their heaviest loads. Front squat and Pendlay row work up to a challenging heavy set.
  • Week 7 (Taper): Drop volume by ~30%. Keep the loads from week 6 — only reduce sets and reps. Box jumps 3×4. Lower: 3×3 FS, 2×6 BSS/RDL. Upper: 3×3 row, 2×reps pull-ups, 2×6 press, 2×12 laterals, 2×reps rings.
  • Week 8 (Race Week): Neural priming only. 1–2 sets of each movement at 60% load. Box jumps 3×3. The goal is to keep the nervous system sharp for race day, not to train. Do not accumulate any fatigue this week.
  • Key rule: Never train lower body strength the day before a hard run. Tuesday lower strength → Wednesday easy run is safe. The easy run flushes soreness without adding intensity stress.

// Lifting Reference

Olympic Lifts, Shoulder & Core

Squat Clean — Key Cues
Wks 1–3: PVC/empty bar. Wks 4–6: load progressively. Wks 7–8: maintain only.
  • First Pull: Push the floor away — don't pull the bar up. Bar stays close to shins. Hips and shoulders rise together.
  • Second Pull: Bar sweeps back into hips. Explosive triple extension — ankles, knees, hips fire simultaneously like a vertical jump.
  • The Catch: Elbows whip forward faster than the bar travels. Receive in front squat. Elbows as high as possible.
  • Front Rack: Bar rests on deltoid shelf, fingers loosely wrapped, elbows parallel to floor. Dropping elbows = crashing bar.
  • Fail signal: Bar crashes, elbows dive, back rounds → strip weight and rebuild from scratch.
Squat Snatch — Key Cues
More technical than the clean. Patience at light weights pays off enormously at heavier loads.
  • Grip Width: Wide — bar at hip crease when hanging at arm's length. Use hook grip (thumb under fingers) once loading begins.
  • Overhead Position: Bar directly over back of neck/traps. Arms fully locked. Active shoulder press into bar — soft elbows = missed lift.
  • The Turnover: Bar goes UP while you go DOWN simultaneously. Punch the bar up and out as you pull yourself under. This is the hardest skill.
  • OHS Catch: Full squat, bar locked overhead, shoulder blades retracted, knees track over toes.
  • Mobility prerequisite: If OHS feels strained, stay on power snatch until ankle/hip/T-spine mobility improves.
Shoulder & Spine — Weighted Work
Done 2× per week at end of lifting sessions. Light loads, full control. Progress conservatively.
  • DB Windmill (3×6/side, 10–20 lbs): DB overhead, hinge laterally. Arm stays locked vertical. Shoulder stability through full lateral range.
  • KB Bottoms-Up Press (3×8/side, 12–16kg): Inverted KB pressed overhead. Forces rotator cuff engagement. Therapeutic for overhead weakness.
  • Landmine Press (3×10/side): 45° angle single-arm press. Trains scapular upward rotation — key for snatch catch. Progress from wk 4 onward.
  • Barbell Good Morning (3×8, empty bar → 50+ lbs): Hinge to parallel, strict neutral spine. Trains erectors under eccentric load. Start empty bar.
  • Jefferson Curl (3×5 slow, 10 lbs → 20–25% BW): Stand on box, flex spine vertebra by vertebra. Best loaded spine mobility drill. Never rush.
  • Suitcase Carry (3×30m/side): Heavy KB in one hand, walk tall. Anti-lateral-flexion. Trains QL — critical for single-leg running stability.
Core Stability Circuit
Anti-movement core work. Resist force, don't create it. Done every lifting session.
  • Weighted Dead Bug (3×8/side, 5–15 lbs): Plates overhead, lower opposing arm/leg. Exhale every rep. Low back flat on floor throughout.
  • Ab Wheel Rollout (3×6–10): Kneel, extend to near-floor, pull back. Anti-extension. Mirrors the bracing demand of the clean and snatch lockout.
  • Pallof Press (3×12/side): Band/cable at chest, press straight out, hold 2 sec. Anti-rotation. Increase resistance wks 4+.
  • Tall Kneeling Press (3×10): Kneel upright, press DB/KB strictly. No leg drive. Forces pure trunk stability. Exposes rib flare under load.
  • Face Pulls (3×15): Pull band to ears with elbows high and wide. External rotation. Do these every single session without exception.
  • Prone Y-T-W (3×8 each, 2.5–5 lbs): Face down on bench. Activates lower trap and posterior rotator cuff — prevents shoulder impingement.

// Mobility Reference

Daily Joint & Tissue Work

Run these 3–4× per week. Pre-lift warm-up is mandatory before every lifting session (12 min). Post-run routine after every run (10 min). Search YouTube for video demos of each exercise.

KOT — Knees Over Toes Guy
ATG = Ass To Grass. Full range is the goal. Bodyweight first, load as range improves.
  • ATG Split Squat (3×8/side): Knee fully over pinky toe, full depth. The single best knee prehab/rehab exercise for runners and lifters alike.
  • Tibialis Raise / Patrick Step (2–3×25, daily): Lean on wall, lift toes. Shin and ankle strength. Prevents shin splints and supports healthy foot strike.
  • Single-Leg ATG Calf Raise (3×15/side): Heel below foot level, 3 sec down. Bulletproofs the Achilles against running volume.
  • Nordic Hamstring Curl (3–5 reps): Eccentric-only hamstring. Protects against hamstring strain from running's high eccentric demand.
  • Couch Stretch (2 min/side): Rear foot on wall, knee toward floor. Restores hip flexor length critical for clean squat depth and running stride.
Strength Side — Full Body Mobility
Search "Strength Side" on YouTube — their hip and thoracic flows are exactly what this program needs.
  • 90/90 Hip Flow (2 min/side, daily): Internal and external hip rotation. Directly transfers to clean squat depth and running stride freedom.
  • Ankle Dorsiflexion Drill (every session): Wall ankle stretch + banded distraction. Non-negotiable before snatching.
  • Deep Squat Hold (work toward 5 min): Assisted with rack or door. Transforms your clean catch position over 8 weeks.
  • T-Spine Foam Roll (every lifting session): Roller perpendicular to spine, T4–T10. Immediately improves overhead position.
  • Shoulder Capsule / Sleeper Stretch: Banded shoulder distraction + sleeper stretch. 90 sec per position per side. Every session.
Shoulder Mobility — Weighted
Passive stretching alone won't build overhead range for the snatch. You must load the range.
  • Shoulder CARs (every session): Maximal slow shoulder circles, everything else still. 5 reps each direction each arm. Builds active range under neural control.
  • Wall Slides + Overpressure (wks 4+): Forearms on wall, slide upward. Add light band resistance. Trains scapular upward rotation — often the missing link in overhead weakness.
  • Cuban Press (5–10 lbs): Upright row → external rotation → overhead press in one movement. Trains the exact rotator cuff pattern of the snatch turnover.
  • Prone Y-T-W (2.5–5 lbs): Activates lower trap and posterior rotator cuff — muscles that prevent shoulder impingement under load.
  • Snatch-Grip Overhead Carry (3×20m): Every overhead stabilizer fires simultaneously. Progress from empty bar to 25 lbs from week 4.
Spine Mobility — Loaded
Train the spine to move under control — not just stretch passively. Load builds tissue tolerance.
  • Thread-the-Needle + Loaded (add 5 lb DB wk 3+): Quadruped thoracic rotation. Unlocks asymmetric T-spine restrictions. 10 reps each side.
  • Kneeling Landmine Rotation (3×8/side): Loaded thoracic rotation for the clean catch and snatch turnover. Control the eccentric.
  • Jefferson Curl (build to 20–25% BW by wk 6): Stand on box, flex spine vertebra by vertebra. Most complete loaded spine mobility drill. Never rush the tempo.
  • Half-Kneeling Banded T-Spine Rotation (3×10/side): Band at chest height. Maximum thoracic rotation range. Do before heavy clean sets.
  • Cat-Cow with Plate (5–10 lbs on back, 2×10): Full flexion-extension cycle under gentle load. Lubricates spinal segments before or after lifting.
